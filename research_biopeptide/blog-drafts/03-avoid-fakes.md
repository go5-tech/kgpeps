# How to Buy Peptides Online in India: Avoiding Fakes and Low Purity

The peptide market in India is flooded with low-quality products, underdosed powders, and outright counterfeits. Indian researchers ordering online face particular risks—some vendors sell peptides claiming 99% purity that are actually 60-70% pure. Others cut corners so drastically that you're paying premium prices for diluted, inactive compounds.

This guide teaches Indian buyers how to spot fake peptides, read real certificates of analysis, understand why HPLC matters, and use a buyer's checklist to protect yourself.

## Why Fake Peptides Exist and How They're Made

Peptide synthesis is expensive. Pure, verified peptides require:
- Chemical synthesis (weeks of lab work)
- HPLC purification
- Third-party testing
- CoA documentation

Total cost per gram: ₹8,000–₹15,000 for legitimate producers.

Scammers skip almost every step:
- Buy cheap, impure peptide precursors from bulk chemical suppliers
- Dilute with inactive fillers (maltose, lactose, cellulose)
- Label as 95-99% pure (with zero testing)
- Sell at 30-50% discount, pocketing the difference

The buyer doesn't know until they try to use it and get zero results.

## Red Flags: How to Spot a Fake or Low-Purity Peptide Before Buying

### Red Flag #1: No HPLC Data Published

**The red flag:** Vendor claims "high purity" but provides zero chromatograms, HPLC charts, or lab reports.

**Why it matters:** HPLC (High-Performance Liquid Chromatography) is the gold standard for peptide purity. A real vendor publishes actual HPLC chromatograms because the test cost is sunk; hiding the results is a sign they don't have them.

**What legitimate data looks like:**
- A PDF or image showing a chromatogram (peak at ~95-99%)
- Lab name and credentials
- Test date and batch number
- Reference to the specific testing methodology

**Red flag phrases:**
- "Purity verified by our in-house lab" (but no actual data)
- "99% pure, guaranteed" (with zero proof)
- "Lab report available upon request" (delay tactic)

### Red Flag #2: Forged or Generic CoA

Real CoA documents are specific to a batch. Fake ones are generic templates with stock numbers filled in.

**How to spot a forged CoA:**

| Real CoA | Forged CoA |
|----------|-----------|
| Batch number matches order | Generic batch number for all products |
| Test date recent (within 2 weeks) | Test date is months old, every product |
| Lab letterhead with phone/address | No contact info for the lab |
| HPLC chart/chromatogram attached | Only a written "purity %" claim |
| Expiry date for test validity (typically 1 year) | No expiry or valid indefinitely |
| Specific gravity, moisture content, assay method | Only purity percentage listed |

**How to verify:** Contact the lab listed on the CoA and ask: "Did you test batch #XYZ on [date]?" A real lab will confirm. A forged one can't.

### Red Flag #3: Inconsistent Product Appearance

Legitimate peptides have consistent color and texture within a batch.

**Warning signs:**
- Powder is dark yellow, brown, or grey (should be white or off-white)
- Crystals instead of powder (improper handling)
- Clumps or moisture (storage problems)
- Powder texture changes between orders (different source or dilution)

Dark colors often indicate oxidation, contamination, or old product.

### Red Flag #5: Vendor Won't Provide Batch-Specific CoA Before Purchase

**Legitimate vendor:** "Here's the CoA for the exact batch you're ordering."

**Scammer:** "We provide CoA after purchase" or "All our batches are identical" or "That information is proprietary."

No legitimate reason exists to withhold batch-specific documentation. This is a major red flag.

### Red Flag #6: Company Has No Physical Address

Legitimate businesses register addresses with tax authorities. If a vendor only has WhatsApp, email, or social media with no verifiable address, they're hiding.

**How to verify:**
- Search their business name on the GST India portal (https://www.gst.gov.in/)
- Call their listed phone number and verify address
- Check if the address appears on multiple platforms (Google Business, website, CoA)

### Red Flag #7: They Won't Accept COD

Requiring 100% prepayment eliminates your recourse if the product is fake or underdosed. Scammers push prepayment because it's irreversible.

Legitimate vendors accept COD because they're confident the product will satisfy you.

## Understanding HPLC: Why It Matters

HPLC testing separates a compound into components and measures each.

**For peptides:**
- The main peak (your peptide) should be 95-99%+
- Side peaks (impurities) should be <5% total
- A chromatogram visually shows this

**Why this matters:** A peptide that's only 70% pure means you're dosing based on incorrect mass. If you use 10mg of "99% pure" that's actually 70% pure, you're getting only 7mg of active peptide.

Your results will be weak or nonexistent.

**Real HPLC data includes:**
- A chromatogram (peak chart)
- Retention time for the compound
- Peak integration percentages
- Testing methodology (reverse-phase HPLC, gradient, solvent system)

If a vendor shows you a CoA with no chromatogram, assume the HPLC wasn't done.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I test a peptide for purity myself at home?**
A: Not reliably. HPLC requires a ₹20–₹50 lakh instrument and expertise. Your best protection is buying from vendors with third-party HPLC CoA and verifying the lab.

**Q: What if the CoA looks real but the lab doesn't exist?**
A: Call the lab phone number. A scammer won't pick up. A real lab will confirm they tested the batch. This is your final verification.

**Q: Is a lower purity peptide (say, 85%) usable?**
A: It depends on your experiment. Lower purity means you're underdosed. If you need exactly 10mg, 85% purity gives you only 8.5mg of active compound. Results suffer.

**Q: How long is a CoA valid for?**
A: Typically 1 year. Peptides can degrade in storage. A CoA older than 1 year is questionable unless storage was perfect.

**Q: Can I tell if a peptide is fake just by dissolving it?**
A: Not visually. A fake peptide might dissolve fine but be inactive. Only HPLC or bioassay (expensive) can confirm purity.
