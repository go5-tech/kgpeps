# What is a Certificate of Analysis (CoA) and Why It Matters for Peptides

A Certificate of Analysis is the document that proves your peptide is actually what the label says it is. Without it, you're trusting the vendor entirely. With it, you have third-party lab verification.

This guide explains what a CoA is, how to read one, what HPLC and MS testing means, and why Peptide Central includes a batch-specific CoA with every order.

## What Exactly Is a Certificate of Analysis?

A Certificate of Analysis (CoA) is a formal document from a testing laboratory that certifies:

1. **Identity** – The compound is actually [peptide name], not something else
2. **Purity** – The percentage of the desired peptide vs. impurities
3. **Quality** – Water content, bacterial endotoxins, sterility, appearance
4. **Testing method** – Which lab equipment and methodology was used

Think of it as a lab's official seal of approval: "We tested this batch. Here's what we found."

### Who Issues a CoA?

A legitimate CoA comes from:
- **Third-party contract labs** – Independent facilities that test pharmaceuticals and chemicals
- **University research labs** – Some research institutions offer testing services
- **Vendor's own lab** – If the vendor operates an internal facility with credentials

The key: It should come from a lab that can be independently verified.

### Why Vendors Should Provide CoA With Every Order

In India, research peptide vendors don't legally *have* to provide CoA. It's an industry standard, not a regulatory requirement. But vendors like Peptide Central include CoA with every order because:

## How to Read a Certificate of Analysis

A typical peptide CoA has these sections:

### Section 1: Product Identification

```
Product Name: MOTS-C (Mitochondrial-Derived Peptide)
Batch Number: PC-MOTSC-2401-15
Lot Code: MOTSC001
Date of Receipt: 2024-01-15
Date of Analysis: 2024-01-17
Analysis Report Date: 2024-01-20
CoA Expiry Date: 2025-01-20
```

**What to check:**
- Batch number matches your order
- Analysis date is recent (within 2 weeks of shipment, ideally)
- CoA is not older than 1 year

### Section 2: Physical Appearance

```
Appearance: White to off-white lyophilized powder
Odor: Characteristic
Consistency: Powder (not crystalline, not clumpy)
```

**What to check:**
- When your powder arrives, color should match
- If it's dark or discolored, alert the vendor immediately

### Section 3: Identity Testing (HPLC)

```
Identity: Confirmed by HPLC
Test Method: Reverse-Phase HPLC with UV detection at 254nm
Retention Time: 12.3 minutes (±0.5 min)
Result: PASS - Consistent with MOTS-C reference standard
```

**What this means:** HPLC proved the compound is actually MOTS-C, not a different peptide or imposter.

**Key phrase:** "Consistent with reference standard" = genuine compound.

### Section 4: Purity (The Most Important Section)

```
Purity Analysis (HPLC): 99.2%
Main Peak Area: 99.2%
Impurities: 0.8%
  - Peak 1 (Unknown): 0.4%
  - Peak 2 (Formyl-MOTS-C): 0.2%
  - Peak 3 (Truncated fragment): 0.2%
Total Impurities: 0.8%
Acceptance Criteria: ≥98%
Result: PASS
```

**What to check:**
- Purity is above 95% (preferably 98%+)
- Impurities are identified (not just lumped as "unknown")
- Acceptance criteria is met

**Red flag:** If a CoA shows 99% purity but lists impurities totaling 5%, the math doesn't add up — forged document.

### Section 5: Water Content (Karl Fischer Titration)

```
Water Content: 2.3% (by Karl Fischer Titration)
Acceptance Criteria: <5.0%
Result: PASS
```

**What to check:**
- Water content is below 5% (preferably below 3%)
- Low water content means better stability and storage longevity
- High water content (>10%) suggests poor handling or storage

### Section 6: Sterility and Safety Testing

```
Bacterial Endotoxins: <10 EU/mg (by LAL test)
Result: PASS

Sterility (Membrane Filtration): Negative for bacteria and fungi
Result: PASS

Heavy Metals (ICP-MS):
  - Lead: <0.1 ppm
  - Mercury: <0.05 ppm
  - Cadmium: <0.05 ppm
  - Arsenic: <0.1 ppm
Result: PASS - All below limits
```

**What to check:**
- Bacterial endotoxins are present but minimal
- No bacterial or fungal growth detected
- Heavy metals are below safety thresholds

### Section 7: Lab Information and Signature

```
Tested By: Dr. Rajesh Kumar, Senior Analyst
Lab Name: PharmAnalytics Testing Laboratory Pvt Ltd
Lab Address: #123 Science Park, Bangalore 560001
Lab Phone: +91-80-1234-5678

Lab Certifications: ISO 17025, GLP certified

Authorized By: Dr. Meena Singh, Laboratory Director
Signature: [Digital/Physical signature]
Date: 2024-01-20
```

**What to check:**
- Lab has ISO 17025 or similar certification
- Contact information is complete
- You can independently call the lab to verify

## Understanding HPLC and Mass Spectrometry Testing

### HPLC (High-Performance Liquid Chromatography)

HPLC is the gold standard for peptide purity testing. Here's what happens:

1. **Sample preparation** – Peptide powder is dissolved in solvent
2. **Injection** – Sample is injected into the HPLC system
3. **Separation** – Different components travel at different speeds through a column
4. **Detection** – UV detector measures each component as it exits
5. **Output** – A chromatogram (peak chart) is generated

**What the chromatogram shows:**
- X-axis: Time (minutes)
- Y-axis: Signal intensity (concentration)
- Tall peak = your peptide (should be 95-99%+)
- Small peaks = impurities

A legitimate CoA includes the actual chromatogram, not just the written result.

**Why it matters:** Chromatograms are hard to fake because they're technical outputs from real instruments. If a vendor shows you one, it's almost certainly genuine.

### Mass Spectrometry (MS)

Some CoAs include MS testing, which is more advanced:

```
Mass Spectrometry (MALDI-TOF):
Theoretical Molecular Weight: 1245.67 Da
Observed Molecular Weight: 1245.68 Da
Accuracy: 99.99%
Result: PASS - Confirms molecular formula
```

**What it means:** MS confirms the exact molecular weight, proving the compound has the correct chemical structure.

**Why it's valuable:** MS is even harder to fake than HPLC and provides absolute proof of identity.

## Common CoA Red Flags

### Red Flag #1: No HPLC Chromatogram

A legitimate CoA includes either:
- The actual chromatogram image/PDF
- A reference to where the chromatogram can be accessed

**Red flag:** CoA says "99% pure" but has no chromatogram. They're guessing.

### Red Flag #2: Generic CoA, Not Batch-Specific

A generic CoA says: "Our typical products are 98% pure."

A batch-specific CoA says: "Batch #PC-MOTSC-2401-15 tested on 2024-01-17 is 99.2% pure."

**Red flag:** Vendor provides the same CoA for all customers. That's not a real test — it's a template.

### Red Flag #3: Acceptance Criteria Not Visible

A good CoA shows: "Purity 99.2%. Acceptance: ≥98%. Result: PASS."

A bad CoA shows: "Purity 95%. [Nothing about what's acceptable]."

If you don't see acceptance criteria, you can't tell if the result is actually acceptable.

### Red Flag #4: Lab Name Not Verifiable

Check if you can find the lab:
- Search on Google Maps
- Call the phone number
- Look them up in GST directory (if India-based)

If the lab doesn't exist, the CoA is forged.

### Red Flag #5: Old CoA

A CoA older than 1 year is less reliable. Peptides can degrade. A recent test (within 3 months) is most trustworthy.

## How to Verify a CoA is Genuine

**Step 1: Check the lab.**
Call the number on the CoA and ask: "Did you test batch #XYZ on [date]?"

A real lab will have records. A fake lab won't answer or will deny it.

**Step 2: Compare to other CoAs from the same lab.**
Legitimate labs have consistent formatting, contact info, and certification numbers. Forged CoAs often have inconsistencies batch-to-batch.

**Step 3: Check for technical detail.**
A real HPLC CoA includes:
- Specific retention times
- Peak integration percentages
- Method details (gradient, column, solvent system)

Generic "99% pure, verified" without technical data is a red flag.

**Step 4: Verify certifications.**
Real testing labs display ISO certifications. A quick search for "Lab Name ISO 17025" should return results.
