# Why 99% Purity Matters in Research Peptides: Understanding CoA Results

Peptide purity is the single most important quality metric in research chemistry. A 95% pure peptide vial contains 5% unknown contaminants that can distort your research findings, cause unexpected side effects, or introduce toxic compounds. This guide explains what "purity" means, why 99.9% is the research standard, how to read a Certificate of Analysis (CoA), and what impurities you should avoid.

## What Is Peptide Purity?

Peptide purity refers to the percentage of the vial's contents that is the intended peptide (e.g., BPC-157) versus everything else. Lower purity means impurities are taking up vial space.

**Example:**
- 5mg vial labeled as BPC-157, 95% purity = 4.75mg actual BPC-157 + 0.25mg impurities
- 5mg vial labeled as BPC-157, 99.9% purity = 4.995mg actual BPC-157 + 0.005mg impurities
- Difference: 0.245mg of unknown contaminants in the 95% vial (5% of dose)

This matters because:
1. **Dosing accuracy breaks down** – You think you're injecting 250 mcg; with 95% purity, you're actually injecting 237.5 mcg (plus 12.5 mcg of unknown substances)
2. **Contaminants can be toxic** – Impurities may be harmful compounds, not benign fillers
3. **Results become unreliable** – Research outcomes depend on precise, pure compounds; impurities skew results

## How Purity Is Tested: HPLC Explained

High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) is the gold standard for purity testing.

### How HPLC Works

1. **Sample injection:** A tiny sample of your peptide vial is dissolved in solvent
2. **Column separation:** The solution is pushed through a specialized column that separates compounds by molecular weight and properties
3. **Detection:** A detector measures each compound as it exits the column, creating a "chromatogram" (a visual graph)
4. **Analysis:** Scientists measure the peak areas; larger peaks = more compound

### Reading an HPLC Chromatogram

An HPLC result shows peaks — each peak is a different compound in the sample.

**Good HPLC (high purity):**
- One dominant peak (your intended peptide, 99%+)
- Tiny trace peaks (impurities <1%)
- Minimal background noise

**Poor HPLC (low purity):**
- Multiple significant peaks (multiple compounds present)
- Dominant peak is <95% of total area
- High background noise (unknown contaminants)

A Certificate of Analysis (CoA) includes the HPLC chromatogram and percentage breakdown.

## What Impurities Can Appear in Peptides?

Research peptides can contain several types of impurities:

### 1. Related Peptides (Most Common)

**What they are:** Incomplete or misfolded versions of the target peptide

**Example:** BPC-157 is a 15-amino acid peptide. If synthesis stops early, you get BPC-142 (incomplete), which is inactive but takes up vial space.

**Risk:** Impure dosing (you think you're injecting 250 mcg BPC-157; you're actually injecting 237 mcg + 13 mcg related peptides)

**Severity:** Medium (not toxic, but reduces efficacy)

### 2. Organic Solvents (Synthesis Byproducts)

**What they are:** Leftover solvents from peptide synthesis (acetonitrile, dimethylformamide, etc.)

**Risk:** Solvent residue can be hepatotoxic (liver damage) at high doses

**Detection:** HPLC will show solvent peaks; CoA should list "solvent residue <X ppm"

**Severity:** High (directly toxic)

### 3. Salts and Minerals (Contamination)

**What they are:** Sodium chloride, potassium, magnesium from contaminated water during synthesis

**Risk:** Can crystallize in reconstituted solution; alters pH; may cause injection site irritation

**Severity:** Medium (local irritation, not systemic toxicity)

### 4. Heavy Metals (Contamination)**

**What they are:** Lead, mercury, cadmium from contaminated raw materials or water

**Risk:** Heavy metal bioaccumulation; neurological and renal toxicity at chronic doses

**Detection:** Not visible on HPLC; requires separate testing (inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry, ICP-MS)

**Severity:** Very High (cumulative toxin)

### 5. Bacterial Endotoxins (Sterilization Failure)

**What they are:** Bacterial lipopolysaccharides from non-sterile manufacturing

**Risk:** Pyrogenic (fever-inducing); immune activation; injection site reactions

**Detection:** Requires LAL (Limulus Amebocyte Lysate) endotoxin test; not shown on HPLC

**Severity:** High (immune response, potential infection)

### 6. Oxidation Byproducts (Age/Storage)**

**What they are:** Degradation products from peptide oxidation over time

**Risk:** Altered efficacy; potential immune activation

**Detection:** HPLC shows additional peaks over time; CoA reflects current (not past) purity

**Severity:** Medium (efficacy loss, not acute toxicity)

## Reading a Certificate of Analysis (CoA)

A proper CoA contains:

### 1. Identifying Information
- **Product name:** BPC-157, Ipamorelin, etc.
- **Lot number:** Batch identifier (always request matching lot)
- **Batch weight:** Total amount produced
- **Test date:** When purity was measured

### 2. Purity Data
- **% Purity (main entry):** Should be 99%+ for research peptides
- **Method:** Should state "HPLC" or "LCMS"
- **Result:** "BPC-157: 99.8%" means 99.8% of the sample is the intended peptide

### 3. HPLC Chromatogram
- **Visual graph:** Shows peaks representing different compounds
- **X-axis:** Time (retention time)
- **Y-axis:** Intensity (amount of compound)
- **Peaks:** One dominant peak (target peptide), minor trace peaks (impurities)

### 4. Impurity Breakdown
- **Related substances:** Percentage of similar compounds
- **Solvent residue:** ppm (parts per million) of organic solvents
- **Water content:** Percentage of water in the sample

### 5. Testing Information
- **Laboratory name:** Certified testing facility (should be recognizable)
- **Analyst signature:** Who performed the test
- **Accreditation:** ISO certifications or equivalent

### Example CoA Analysis

**GOOD CoA:**
```
Product: BPC-157
Purity: 99.8% (HPLC)
Related Substances: <0.2%
Solvent Residue: <10 ppm
Water Content: <1%
Heavy Metals: <10 ppb
Endotoxins: <0.25 EU/mg
Laboratory: ISO 17025 Accredited Facility
Date: 2026-05-15
```

**Interpretation:** This is research-grade. 99.8% purity means you're getting nearly pure peptide. Solvent residue <10 ppm is safe. Heavy metals and endotoxins are below concern thresholds.

**POOR CoA:**

## Storage and Purity Over Time

Purity degrades if not stored properly:

**Temperature impact:**
- 2-8°C (refrigerator): Minimal degradation, stable 12-24 months
- 15-25°C (room temp): Moderate degradation, stable 3-6 months
- >25°C (warm): Rapid degradation, stable weeks only

**Light impact:**
- Dark vial storage: Protective
- Transparent container, sunny window: Rapid degradation (UV breaks peptide bonds)

**Opened vials:**
- Reconstituted peptides: Use within 2-4 weeks (even refrigerated)
- Powder vials: Use within 24 months if sealed and refrigerated
