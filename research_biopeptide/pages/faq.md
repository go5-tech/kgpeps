## What is a Certificate of Analysis (COA)?

A Certificate of Analysis (COA) is an official laboratory report that certifies the identity, purity, and quality of a peptide sample. It documents:
- **Peptide identity** (confirmed via mass spectrometry)
- **Purity percentage** (determined by HPLC testing)
- **Sterility testing** (if applicable)
- **Endotoxin levels** (for injectable-grade products)
- **Batch number and manufacturing date**
- **Testing laboratory name and credentials**

The COA is proof that your peptide has been independently tested and verified to meet research-grade standards. It's essential for peer-reviewed research, regulatory compliance, and quality assurance.

## What does 99.9% purity mean?

**99.9% purity** means that 99.9% of your peptide sample is the intended, active peptide compound. The remaining 0.1% consists of minor impurities such as water, salts, or trace byproducts from synthesis. This is verified through **HPLC (High-Performance Liquid Chromatography)** testing. For research purposes, 99.9% purity is the gold standard—it ensures your experimental results are reliable and not confounded by contaminants. Peptide Central guarantees 99.9% purity (or higher) for all products, verified by third-party labs.

## What is HPLC testing?

**HPLC (High-Performance Liquid Chromatography)** is the industry standard analytical method for measuring peptide purity. Here's how it works:
1. Your peptide sample is dissolved and injected into the HPLC machine
2. The liquid mixture is pushed through a specialized column under high pressure
3. Different compounds in the mixture separate based on their chemical properties
4. A detector measures how much of each compound is present
5. The result is a chromatogram—a graph showing the purity percentage

HPLC can detect impurities as small as 0.01%, making it the most reliable way to verify peptide quality. All Peptide Central products are HPLC-tested by independent third-party laboratories before shipment.

## How should I store peptides?

Proper storage ensures peptide stability and effectiveness:
- **Lyophilized peptides:** Store in a cool, dry place (2-8°C in a refrigerator or freezer is ideal; room temperature is acceptable for short-term storage)
- **Reconstituted peptides:** Store reconstituted peptides in the refrigerator (2-8°C) and use within 2-4 weeks, or freeze at -20°C for longer storage
- **Avoid:** Direct sunlight, heat, humidity, and repeated freezing/thawing cycles
- **Keep sealed:** Always keep your peptide vials sealed when not in use to prevent degradation

Most research-grade peptides remain stable for 12-24 months if stored correctly. Your COA will list the specific storage recommendations for your peptide.

## How do I reconstitute lyophilized peptides?

Reconstitution is the process of dissolving freeze-dried peptides back into solution for use:
1. **Gather supplies:** Sterile syringe, needle, bacteriostatic water (0.9% saline solution with benzyl alcohol), and your peptide vial
2. **Calculate volume:** Decide your desired concentration (e.g., 5 mg/mL)
3. **Draw bacteriostatic water:** Use a sterile syringe to draw the calculated volume of bacteriostatic water
4. **Inject slowly:** Slowly inject the water into your sealed peptide vial
5. **Let it dissolve:** Allow 5-10 minutes for the peptide to fully dissolve—do not shake vigorously
6. **Store reconstituted solution:** Keep refrigerated (2-8°C) and use within 2-4 weeks

Always use sterile, non-pyrogenic water (bacteriostatic water) to prevent contamination and bacterial growth. Never use distilled water alone—it lacks preservatives.

## What is bacteriostatic water and do you sell it?

**Bacteriostatic water** is sterile, pyrogen-free water containing 0.9% benzyl alcohol as a preservative. It prevents bacterial and fungal growth in reconstituted peptide solutions, making it ideal for research applications. It has a shelf life of 30 days once opened and must be stored in a cool place.

## What is the difference between Retatrutide and Tirzepatide?

Both **Retatrutide** and **Tirzepatide** are GLP-1 receptor agonists used in clinical research, but they differ in their mechanism and development stage:

**Retatrutide:**
- Triple GLP-1/GIP/Glucagon receptor agonist
- Still in clinical trial phase (Phase 3)
- Designed to activate three hormonal pathways for potentially greater metabolic effects
- Newer compound with limited long-term human data
- Developed by Eli Lilly

**Tirzepatide:**
- Dual GLP-1/GIP receptor agonist
- Already FDA-approved for clinical use (marketed as Zepbound for weight management)
- Proven efficacy and safety profile from extensive clinical trials
- More established in research literature
- Also developed by Eli Lilly
